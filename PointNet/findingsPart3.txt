Part 3.1:
In the first subset, one spatial dimension was randomly removed from each sample, 
effectively projecting the 3D point cloud into a 2D plane. Training the model on this data resulted in a noticeable decrease in classification performance. 
This indicates that PointNet strongly relies on the full 3D structure of objects, and losing even a single coordinate reduces its ability to capture meaningful geometric relationships between points.

In the second subset, random rotations were applied followed by a perspective projection along a random dimension. 
This transformation introduced both viewpoint variation and geometric distortion. The model showed further performance degradation compared to the original dataset, 
suggesting that perspective distortion affects the relative spatial arrangement of points and makes feature learning more difficult. 
Although PointNet is designed to be somewhat invariant to rotations (via the T-Net), large distortions still negatively impact performance.

Overall, these experiments demonstrate that PointNet depends heavily on accurate 3D spatial information, 
and that removing dimensions or applying strong projections reduces the quality of learned features.

Part 3.2:
After training the modified model on the dataset, the results showed that reducing the network size slightly decreased the model’s ability to capture complex geometric features, 
which led to a small drop in classification accuracy. The additional hidden layer partially compensated for this reduction by allowing deeper feature representation.

Replacing ReLU with LeakyReLU improved training stability by preventing dead neurons and allowing gradient flow for negative inputs. However, 
the most significant change was replacing max pooling with average pooling. Max pooling selects the strongest local feature, 
while average pooling aggregates information from all points. As a result, average pooling produced smoother global representations but slightly reduced the model’s sensitivity to 
distinctive structural features.

Overall, the modified architecture demonstrated that PointNet’s performance is sensitive to feature dimensionality and pooling strategy, 
and that max pooling remains particularly effective for capturing dominant geometric patterns.

The transformation matrices predicted by each network were applied directly to the point coordinates before visualization.

The results showed that both networks learned meaningful spatial transformations that aligned the point clouds into more canonical orientations. 
The baseline PointNet typically produced transformations that focused on stabilizing rotation and scale. 
The modified network often produced slightly stronger or more irregular transformations due to the reduced feature size and different pooling strategy, 
but it still successfully normalized object orientation.

These findings confirm that the T-Net successfully learns spatial alignment transformations and plays an important role in improving the invariance of PointNet to geometric variations.
